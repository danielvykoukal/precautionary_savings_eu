## 1. Europe Is Saving Out of Fear

**Charts:** `A_saving_vs_uncertainty.png`, `O3_europe_saving_map.png`, `E5_saving_intentions.png`

The euro-area household saving rate has been stuck near 14–15% for three years —
well above the 12–13% households saved before the pandemic. The COVID spike to
18.3% made sense: lockdowns closed the shops, so income that couldn't be spent
piled up as forced saving. But that shock unwound years ago, incomes and wealth
have normalised, and the rate never fully came back down. Something else is
holding it up.

The euro area's own central bank has an answer: precautionary saving. A January
2026 ECB analysis found that households who report more uncertainty about the
future also report lower realised spending and higher realised saving — exactly
what buffer-stock theory predicts. When the future looks riskier, people defer
big purchases and rebuild a cash cushion "just in case." The timing fits too:
the second climb in the saving rate lines up almost exactly with February 2022 —
the invasion of Ukraine and the energy-price shock that followed, both visible as
spikes in geopolitical-risk indices plotted against the saving rate.

The caution isn't evenly spread across the map. Germany saves close to 20% of
disposable income, among the highest of any large economy; France isn't far
behind. Spain (under 13%) and Italy (around 12%) sit well below the EU average —
still above their own pre-pandemic norms, but clearly less spooked. The
countries most exposed to the Russia/energy shock — Germany, Austria, the
Baltics, the CEE bloc — do skew toward the high-saving end, though a chunk of
that gap is just long-standing habit: Germans saved more than Italians long
before 2022. The honest read is a modest, positive effect of the shock layered
on top of an old structural map.

Survey evidence on saving *intentions* backs the story up from a different
angle: households aren't just ending up with more saved by accident, they say
they mean to keep a bigger buffer.

None of this is proof of a single causal channel — interest rates rose at the
same time as uncertainty, and both push saving up, so the charts show
correlation, not an isolated precautionary effect. But the pattern is
consistent and it matters: a European household that saves more out of fear
spends less on the margin, and that's a quiet, self-reinforcing drag on growth
and on how far a rate cut actually reaches the real economy. Rational for each
household. Costly once 300 million of them do it at once.

## 2. It's Not Cash Under the Mattress

**Charts:** `G_liquidity_ladder_stocks.png`, `G2_liquidity_ladder_flows.png`, `F4_tilt_vs_saving.png`

Picture the "precautionary buffer" story and most people picture cash: euros
sitting in a current account, doing nothing, ready for an emergency. By that
measure, European households look *less* precautionary since 2022 — the
share of new financial saving going straight into instant-access cash and
overnight deposits collapsed from 66% (2015–19) to just 2% in the years since.
Case closed, caution is fading?

Not quite. Cash is not the only thing you can turn into money in an
emergency. Rank household assets by how fast they can actually be sold —
instant cash, then near-money like short notice deposits, then "marketable"
holdings like bonds, listed shares and fund units that can be liquidated in
days, and only then genuinely illiquid things like unlisted business equity
and pension entitlements — and a different picture appears. Only about 18% of
euro-area household financial wealth is literally cash. But roughly half is
sellable within days once you count the broader liquid tier.

And the flow story flips too. Yes, the cash share of new saving fell off a
cliff. But the *broad* liquid share of new saving — cash plus near-money plus
marketable securities — actually rose, from 54% before the shock to 73% after
it. Households didn't abandon their buffers when rates rose; they moved the
buffer up the yield curve, out of a 0%-interest current account and into term
deposits, short bonds and fund shares that still convert to cash in days if
needed. "Out of cash" turned out to mean "into something almost as liquid, but
paying interest" — not "out of the precautionary buffer."

The macro money supply tells the same story from the other side: as rates
rose, roughly €1.2 trillion moved out of overnight M1 deposits into
term/notice deposits and marketable near-money instruments between 2022 and
2024. This is households — and the banking system — reorganising *how* they
hold their caution, not reducing it. A saving rate built purely on the
cash share would have badly understated how prepared European households
actually are for a shock, and overstated how much their appetite for safety
has faded.

## 3. Why Did Europeans Start Saving More? Read It Straight From the Accounts

**Charts:** `M5_savings_reconciliation_why.png`, `M4_savings_reconciliation_validation.png`, `M2_savings_reconciliation_decomposition.png`

Most saving-rate stories start from the ratio — saving divided by income — and
argue about what moved it. This one starts from the actual euro-area sector
accounts: every euro of income, every euro of consumption, every deposit,
bond, insurance contract and mortgage households acquired or repaid. Rebuilt
bottom-up from Eurostat's national accounts, that bottom-up build reproduces
the officially reported saving rate to within a tenth of a percentage point
across two decades. That's not a coincidence — it's a licence to trust the
decomposition of *why* the rate moved, not just that it moved.

Two distinct episodes, two different mechanisms. The 2020–21 COVID spike to
18.3% was a pure consumption shock: disposable income was essentially flat
(+0.1%) while consumption collapsed (–7.2%) because lockdowns physically
closed the spending channel. The arithmetic — saving equals one minus
consumption over income — did the rest. Almost all of that windfall went
straight into financial assets, doubling from 7.5% to 13.9% of income, and
overwhelmingly into instant cash. This was forced saving, not a change of
heart.

The durable step that followed — settling near 14.4%, about two points above
the pre-pandemic norm — has two separate legs. First, nominal income kept
slightly ahead of consumption as inflation ran hot. Second, and more
strikingly, borrowing collapsed: net new household debt fell from 2.8% to
1.9% of income as the ECB's hiking cycle made credit expensive, with new
mortgage lending dropping from €280bn in 2022 to just €39bn in 2023. Financing
more of your life out of your own income, rather than borrowed money,
mechanically raises the net saving rate — no change of heart required either.

One myth the accounts kill outright: housing didn't drive any of this.
Housing investment sat rock-steady around 9% of income through COVID, the
energy shock and the rate-hiking cycle alike. The swing came from consumption
behaviour and then from the credit channel — not from households pouring
money into property or, on the flip side, running down their old buffers into
bricks and mortar.

## 4. It's Not That Europeans Got Richer

**Charts:** `N_real_consumption_function.png`, `N2_real_income_consumption.png`

A tempting, boring explanation for a higher saving rate is simply: incomes
rose, and richer people save a bigger share of a bigger pie. Deflate the euro
area's nominal numbers by actual prices, and that explanation falls apart.

Between 2019 and 2023, euro-area household disposable income rose 21.2% in
nominal terms — but only 3.8% once you strip out inflation. Consumption grew
19.6% nominal, 2.4% real. Real income did rise, just barely. To see whether
that small real gain can explain a higher saving rate, fit the historical
relationship between real consumption and real income using two decades of
pre-COVID data (2002–2019): consumption tracks income almost one-for-one
(a marginal propensity to consume near 0.94), with an intercept close to
zero. That shape matters — it means the average European household's
propensity to consume does *not* fall as its income grows. So "people got a
bit richer, and richer households save more of each euro" isn't the
mechanism; if anything, modest real income growth alone would nudge the
saving rate down slightly, not up.

What actually happened is that post-2022 consumption sits visibly *below*
what that decades-old income–consumption relationship would predict — and the
gap keeps widening, from roughly €90bn short of the historical line in 2022
to about €220bn short by 2025. A formal structural-break test confirms the
consumption function itself shifted after 2021, not just wobbled. Splitting
the 2022–25 saving rate into "what real income alone would predict" (about
12.1%) versus the actual rate (14.4%) leaves a genuine behavioural residual of
about 2.3 percentage points that real income growth cannot explain.

That residual is the closest thing in the whole project to hard evidence of a
behavioural shift, not just an accounting one: households are choosing to
consume less out of each euro of real income than their own pre-pandemic
habits would predict, and that choice has persisted — and grown — for three
years running. It doesn't prove the shift is *only* precaution — slow
unwinding of COVID buffers, relative energy prices, and higher returns on
saving are all tangled in there too — but "Europeans just got richer" is now
off the table.

## 5. Is 15% the New Normal?

**Charts:** `O2_structural_vs_cyclical.png`, `O_saving_rate_evolution.png`

Every elevated economic indicator eventually faces the same question: is this
a cycle that will fade, or a new baseline? Split the euro-area saving rate
into a slow-moving trend and a cyclical deviation from it (excluding the 2020
lockdown quarters, which were a one-off shock rather than part of any trend),
and the answer is unambiguous: the trend itself has stepped up, from about
12.5% in the 2012–19 period to roughly 15.1% today. Meanwhile the *cycle* —
how far the rate sits from that new trend right now — is close to zero. In
plain terms: the saving rate isn't elevated relative to some temporary shock
still working its way out of the system. The rate simply *is* its new normal,
already.

That distinction matters enormously for what comes next. A cyclical
overshoot predicts reversion — consumption should eventually catch up as the
shock fades, offering a tailwind to growth and to monetary policy
transmission. A structural step predicts the opposite: this is the level
households have settled on, and there's no mechanical reason to expect it to
fall back toward 12–13% just because time passes.

Why would the norm itself move? The likeliest structural candidates are ones
that don't fade with the calendar: a public sense that pay-as-you-go pensions
will cover less of retirement than they used to, elevated geopolitical
tension that shows no sign of resolving, and a genuine, lasting shift in
household caution that outlived the shocks that triggered it (see post #4).
None of those unwind on their own schedule.

For anyone forecasting euro-area consumption, or for a central bank wondering
how much extra transmission a rate cut buys, the honest takeaway from this
chart is: stop waiting for saving to "normalise" back to the old level.
Fifteen percent, roughly, *is* the new normal — plan around it rather than
around its reversal.

## 6. Cautious Holders, Not Low Savers: Europe vs the US

**Charts:** `L_us_vs_ea_saving.png`, `L2_us_vs_ea_ladder.png`, `L3_us_vs_ea_caution.png`

Ask most people whether Americans or Europeans save more, and the intuitive
answer — Europeans, obviously, they're the cautious ones — is only half
right, and the interesting half is the one usually left out.

The real, persistent transatlantic gap isn't in *how much* households save
out of income; it's in *how* they hold what they've saved. US households keep
roughly 57% of their financial wealth in equities and investment funds.
Euro-area households keep about 37% — one and a half times less market
exposure. Flip the picture and it's a mirror image: American households hold
around 11% of their wealth in bank deposits, euro-area households around 31%,
nearly three times as much sitting in cash-like instruments earning little.

This isn't a recent divergence born of the 2022 energy shock or the war in
Ukraine — it's a two-decade-old, remarkably stable gap that long predates
either. Which points to the real explanation: institutions, not temperament.
The US built a funded, equity-heavy private retirement system — 401(k)s and
IRAs — that effectively forces tens of millions of households to hold stock
market exposure through their own retirement savings, alongside a much older
culture of direct equity investing. Continental Europe, by contrast, leaned
for decades on pay-as-you-go state pensions, where the state (not the
household's own balance sheet) bears the market risk, and on a banking system
built around deposit-taking rather than brokerage.

The consequence is that European households look "risk-averse" largely
because their pension architecture never required them to hold risk in the
first place — not necessarily because they're more anxious about the future
than Americans are. It's a structural, plumbing-level difference dressed up
as a psychological one.

That reframing has a sharper edge for policy than it might first appear:
Europe's low equity culture means European households capture less of every
equity bull market, and it means a policy push toward funded pensions — which
several EU governments are actively discussing — would, almost mechanically,
push European portfolios to look more American, whether or not household
psychology changes at all.

## 7. Goods on a Diet, Services in Full Swing

**Chart:** `O4_goods_vs_services.png`

Here's a puzzle that trips up anyone reading the headline numbers: the
euro-area saving rate has been unusually high for three years, yet by every
account restaurants are full, flights are booked, and consumer-facing
services businesses report some of their strongest years on record. High
saving is supposed to mean households pulling back on spending across the
board. So which is it?

Both, just split by category. Look at real consumption *volumes* — quantities
actually bought, prices stripped out — indexed to 2019, and goods and
services tell almost opposite stories. Goods spending boomed during lockdown
(nowhere else to put the money) and then went essentially flat, sitting only
a few points above its pre-pandemic level years later. Services spending did
the reverse: it collapsed in 2020 (you cannot fly, dine out, or get a haircut
under lockdown) and then didn't just recover, it kept climbing, running
comfortably above its old trend.

So the elevated saving rate and the strong services economy are not actually
in tension. Households rotated their spending, they didn't uniformly cut it.
The generous slice of extra caution shows up almost entirely in the goods
basket — the durable purchases, the big-ticket discretionary buys, the
things that are easy to defer when the future looks uncertain — while the
appetite for experiences, once physically available again, came roaring
back and then some, helped along by services inflation running hotter than
goods inflation on the nominal side.

It's a small chart with a large implication for how to read "high saving"
headlines going forward: aggregate consumption growth can look healthy, and
consumer-facing sectors can boom, even while the household saving rate stays
elevated — because the caution isn't spread evenly across the shopping cart.
Anyone forecasting durable-goods demand or discretionary retail should treat
this rotation, not the aggregate saving rate, as the more diagnostic number.

## 8. What Actually Moves the Saving Rate? Spreads, Not Sentiment

**Charts:** `P_panel_coefficients.png`, `P_nowcast_bridge.png`

Everyone has a favourite explanation for why household saving moves — fear,
sentiment, inflation, unemployment expectations. Put sixteen euro-area
countries, twenty-six years of quarterly data, and every one of those
candidate explanations into the same statistical horse race, controlling for
both the country and the point in time, and one variable comes out
decisively ahead of the rest: the sovereign bond spread.

Where a country's government borrowing costs sit relative to Germany's turns
out to be the single robust, statistically reliable predictor of household
saving across this entire panel — and the sign runs opposite to the simple
"fear raises saving" story. Households in fiscally stressed economies, where
spreads are wide, save *less*, not more. That is worth sitting with: it says
financial stress — the kind that threatens jobs, credit availability and
incomes directly — dominates any parallel urge to build a precautionary
cushion. When a country's own government looks shaky, households don't
respond by squirreling money away; they're too busy protecting themselves
from the more immediate squeeze.

Survey measures of saving intentions and expected unemployment do carry the
sign precaution theory predicts — more worried households say they intend to
save more — but with only sixteen countries in the panel, those effects are
imprecisely estimated, not statistically decisive on their own.

There's a genuinely useful, if humbler, payoff on the forecasting side too.
A simple model combining survey saving intentions with money-supply growth
explains a respectable 71% of quarterly variation in the saving rate and
beats naive forecasting benchmarks by a wide margin — but almost entirely
because it can see turning points like 2020 coming. Strip out the COVID
years and it's roughly tied with just assuming next quarter looks like this
quarter. The honest conclusion is a two-speed one: in calm times the
saving rate is so persistent that no model beats "no change"; survey and
monetary leads earn their keep only when something is about to break.
