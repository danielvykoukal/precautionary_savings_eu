##########################################################################
# Real terms — is the higher saving rate just 'more income'? (consumption fn)
##########################################################################

Nominal vs REAL growth (deflated by the consumption deflator):
transition      nom inc  nom cons  REAL inc  REAL cons
2019->2022       +12.8     +12.2      +2.6       +2.1
2019->2023       +21.2     +19.6      +3.8       +2.4
2019->2025       +30.9     +28.1      +7.3       +5.0
  -> the +21% nominal income (2019->23) is mostly inflation: real income rose only a few percent.

Pre-COVID real consumption function (2002-2019): C = -442 + 0.938*Y   (MPC=0.938, R2=0.975)
  intercept is ~0/negative -> the average propensity to consume does NOT fall as real income grows, so 'richer -> saves a bigger share' does not apply here. Pure real-income growth would, if anything, nudge the saving rate DOWN.

Post-shock 2022-2025: actual vs pre-COVID-predicted consumption (EUR bn, 2015 prices):
 year    realY    realC    predC     gap  actual s%  on-curve s%
 2022    7,337    6,350    6,440     -90      13.6%        12.2%
 2023    7,422    6,369    6,519    -150      14.0%        12.2%
 2024    7,596    6,453    6,683    -230      15.0%        12.0%
 2025    7,671    6,532    6,753    -221      14.9%        12.0%

Saving-rate decomposition, 2022-2025 avg:
  actual saving rate                  14.4%
  predicted by income alone (on-curve) 12.1%   <- where real income growth alone would put it
  => behavioural shift component       +2.3 pp   (households consume LESS than their historical income relationship)
  (pre-COVID mean saving rate 12.9%)

Chow break test (split at 2022): F = 11.63, k=2, n=22 -> a clear structural break in the consumption function.

VERDICT: real income did rise modestly, but the pre-COVID relationship would put the saving rate ~12% (flat-to-down). The actual ~15% comes from consumption sitting BELOW its historical link to income — a genuine downward shift, and it WIDENS through 2025 (persistent, not reverting). So the higher saving rate is NOT just 'people have more money'; it is consistent with a structural rise in (precautionary) saving.
